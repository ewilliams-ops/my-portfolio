Executive Summary

PlanHub's Network Intelligence system monitors trade hunger signals across U.S. metros monthly — identifying markets where subcontractor supply has outpaced project opportunity demand. This report cross-references those signals against the TAM/GCAS ticket pipeline to answer a critical question: is the pipeline aligned with where demand signals are actually firing?

The answer, as of April 2026, is largely no. The NI system detected active hunger signals across 326 unique CBSAs. The TAM/GCAS pipeline holds 2,379 tickets — but only 84 of those tickets (3.5%) are in markets where hunger signals are currently active. The remaining 96.5% of the pipeline has no corresponding demand signal, and 242 markets with active signals have no pipeline presence at all.

Section 2
Scoring Methodology

Each month, the data warehouse produces a CBSA × trade snapshot. A hunger signal fires on any row where the number of active subcontractors in a rolling 3-month window exceeds available project opportunities — meaning there is more SC capacity than work to fill it.

Markets are scored by accumulating points across all trade rows within a CBSA, then applying a multiplier for core markets. The resulting score determines which HubSpot TAM/GCAS ticket to prioritize.

Core trade  ·  hunger_state = 2 (Very Hungry)2 pts

Hunger state is derived from the trade_hunger_ratio field. A ratio above 4.0 is classified as state 2; between 1.0–4.0 as state 1; below 1.0 as balanced (state 0). Non-core trades receive partial credit to surface demand gaps in markets where core-classified trades are not the primary capacity constraint.

Conceptual Framework
The Conceptual Framework — Why Non-Core Trade Signals Matter

In WWII, Abraham Wald was asked to help the military decide where to reinforce bomber aircraft. Analysts studied planes that returned from missions and found bullet holes concentrated on the wings and fuselage — so they planned to armor those areas. Wald pointed out the critical flaw: you are only seeing the planes that survived. Aircraft hit in the engine never returned to be studied, so those areas showed zero damage. The absence of bullet holes on the engine was not evidence of safety — it was evidence that those hits were fatal. The right answer was to reinforce where you saw no damage.

This is the insight that shaped our scoring design. A model measuring only core-trade hunger would treat Atlanta, San Francisco, Miami, Nashville, and New York as balanced markets — when those cities have meaningful supply-demand gaps across non-core trades that an incomplete model never sees. The absence of a core-trade signal is not evidence of a healthy market. It means we are not measuring all the right dimensions. By including non-core trade signals, our methodology captures the full picture of where SC capacity is falling short of project demand.

WWII Context
● Bullet holes on wings & fuselage — visible damage on planes that survived and returned
○ No holes on engines — those planes were shot down; the data never came back to be counted
→ Wald's insight: reinforce the engines — not the bullet-riddled wings
→
Trade Hunger Model
● Core trade signals — what the baseline counts and scores in HubSpot today
○ Non-core trade hunger in large CBSAs — invisible to baseline; Atlanta, SF, Miami, NYC all score zero despite real demand gaps
→ Our model: includes non-core trade signals — surfaces all 22 core CBSAs, including Atlanta, SF, Miami, NYC
Methodology Note

Non-core trades receive partial credit by design — not as a compromise, but to ensure broad market coverage without over-indexing on narrow trade classifications. This prevents the survivorship bias described above from hiding demand gaps in core markets where non-core trades dominate the capacity shortfall. Multiple scoring configurations were evaluated during development. Full scenario comparison is available on request.

Production Insights — What We're Looking to Answer

This analysis was run against a sandbox environment. The methodology is validated — what we don't yet know is what the production numbers look like. Below are the specific business questions this integration is designed to answer once deployed against the full production pipeline.

Pipeline Alignment
What is the true overlap between NI hunger signals and our full production pipeline?

Sandbox showed 3.5%. Production will tell us whether that gap is consistent or whether the sandbox sample skewed the picture. This becomes our baseline pipeline health metric.

Coverage Gap
How many markets are we missing — signals firing with no ticket in the pipeline?

In sandbox, 242 signal markets had no pipeline presence. In production, this number may be significantly larger — and represents the most actionable outreach opportunity we can identify from data.

Priority Distribution
Of the tickets that do match, how many surface as High or Critical priority?

Sandbox showed 27 of 84 matched tickets at High or Critical (32%). Does production hold that ratio, or does a broader pipeline surface more — or fewer — genuinely urgent markets?

Pipeline Quality
Are there tickets in the pipeline for markets with no demand signal backing them up?

96.5% of sandbox tickets had no corresponding signal. Understanding whether those tickets represent genuine opportunities — or pipeline noise — is a direct input into how we qualify and prioritize outreach.

Trend & Repeatability
How does signal coverage as a pipeline health metric trend month over month?

Running this analysis monthly gives us a time series: is the overlap rate improving as the team acts on signals? Are new markets entering the Critical tier? This turns a one-time snapshot into a continuous feedback loop.

Trade-Level Intelligence
Which specific trades are driving the most hunger signals — and are we recruiting for them?

Sandbox surfaced Demolition & Site Construction and Electrical & Low Voltage as dominant hungry trades. Production will validate whether these are systematic gaps or regional patterns — directly informing SC recruitment prioritization.
